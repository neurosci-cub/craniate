The Rubber Hand Illusion

This powerful illusion demonstrates how our sense of body ownership can be manipulated, causing you to feel sensations in an object that's not even part of your body. It reveals how our perception of our physical self is constructed by our brain and can be surprisingly flexible.

The Science Behind It:

This illusion works through multisensory integration. Your brain receives visual input (seeing the brush stroke the rubber hand) and tactile input (feeling the brush on your real hand) simultaneously. Because these sensory inputs are synchronized, your brain creates a coherent but false perception that the rubber hand is part of your body.

This activity distorts body ownership, the ability for a brain to recognize (and use) its own body parts. When the fake hand and the real hand both receive tactile (touch) information that is agrees with visual information, your brain will start to incorporate the fake hand as a body part. The longer the visual-tactile stimulus happens, the stronger the feeling of ownership.
Mirror neurons are cells in our brain that send signals when we see another person moving or sensing an input. These cells help us feel what other people feel (like when someone stubs a toe) help us determine if a body part is our own or someone else’s. During this illusion, mirror neurons become quiet, meaning the false hand is sensed as our own.
