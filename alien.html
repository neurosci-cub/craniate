The Experiment

Sometimes, your brain takes in sensory information layer by layer to piece together what’s happening. This is called “bottom-up processing.” Once your brain knows what is being said, it applies the information and makes sense of the distorted sound. More often - as is happening in this activity - your brain operates using “top-down processing,” making predictions based on previous knowledge. Brains do this to save time, and because each person’s prior experiences are unique, top-down processing can cause people to perceive the same information in different ways.

This illusion happens because of top-down (or memory-driven) processing in the brain, and from the sound itself. “Yanny” is in higher frequencies (or pitches) which we see on the right, while “laurel” is in the low frequencies (left). You can see how the original recording (middle) looks like a combination of left and right sounds.

As we age, it’s common that we start losing our ability to hear the higher frequency sounds. It wouldn’t be surprising if someone younger hears Yanny while someone older listening through the same device hears Laurel. Not all speakers are made equal, so depending on the quality of sound, some may hear Yanny while others hear Laurel.
